The hooves thing is a persistent myth, actually. Glue doesn't come from hooves — it comes from collagen, which is the protein packed into skin, bones, and connective tissue. Hooves are made of keratin (same stuff as hair and fingernails), and keratin makes lousy glue. So the classic image of "off to the glue factory" being about hooves gets the chemistry backwards.

Here's the actual traditional process, roughly:

The useful raw material is hide, bones, tendons, ligaments, cartilage — the connective bits. Historically, when an old or sick horse was put down, the whole animal might get rendered, but different parts went to different products (fat for tallow, meat for other uses, etc.). The collagen-rich tissue is what became glue.

To extract it, you do essentially a long, low simmer. Collagen is a tough, triple-helix protein, and prolonged exposure to hot water breaks it down and unwinds it into gelatin — which is just partially hydrolyzed collagen. (Yes, the same gelatin as in Jell-O and gummy bears; the difference between "animal glue" and "food gelatin" is mostly purity and processing, not fundamental chemistry.) Traditionally the tissue might first get a lime or acid soak to help break things down and strip away non-collagen gunk.

Once you've cooked out the collagen and gotten a gooey liquid, you filter it, concentrate it by boiling off water, and let it cool and dry. As it cools it sets into a gel; dried out, it becomes hard sheets or granules. To use it, you reheat it with water — hence the traditional hide glue pot, kept warm so the glue stays liquid while you work. It's still used today in things like violin-making and antique furniture restoration, partly because it's reversible with heat and moisture, which is great for repairs.

So the short answer to your thought experiment: not the hooves, and not magically the "whole horse" either — it's the collagen in skin and connective tissue, converted to gelatin by long cooking. The horse is somewhat incidental; cattle were and are far more common as the source, which is why "hide glue" usually means cowhide.

Ha, okay, I respect the commitment to a bit. Here's how you'd actually industrialize it — this is real machinery, the gelatin and rendering industries run exactly this kind of pipeline:

Intake and size reduction. First stop is a grinder or crusher — industrial bone crushers and hide cutters break raw material down into small, uniform pieces. Smaller pieces mean more surface area, which means faster collagen extraction downstream. Think heavy-duty augers and rotary cutters, the kind of thing that sounds like a wood chipper's angrier cousin.

Degreasing / rendering. Before you extract collagen you want the fat out, because fat contaminates the glue. This is a rendering step: material goes into a cooker (a steam-jacketed vessel or a continuous rendering system) that melts fat away, and a centrifuge or press separates the tallow from the solids. Continuous rendering systems move everything along on screw conveyors so nothing has to be loaded by hand.

Pretreatment (the chemistry soak). The degreased solids go into big tanks for the acid or alkaline (lime) soak that loosens the collagen and strips non-collagen proteins. Automated version = large agitated tanks with dosing pumps metering in the acid/base, pH probes feeding a controller, and timed transfers. This is the slow, boring, tank-farm part.

Extraction. Pretreated material moves into extraction vessels — essentially big temperature-controlled tanks where hot water pulls the collagen out as it converts to gelatin. Industrially this is often done in multiple stages at rising temperatures, each stage pulling another fraction. Pumps and heat exchangers move and heat the liquor between stages.

Filtration and clarification. The raw gelatin liquor is full of fine solids, so it runs through filters (plate-and-frame presses, or in modern plants, ultrafiltration membranes) and sometimes centrifuges to clarify it.

Concentration (evaporation). Now you've got dilute gelatin in a lot of water. Multiple-effect vacuum evaporators boil the water off at low temperature — low temp matters because you don't want to cook the gelatin further and degrade it. Out comes a thick, concentrated syrup.

Drying. The concentrate is chilled so it sets into a gel, extruded into noodles or ribbons, and run through a drying tunnel — a long belt with progressively conditioned air blowing over it. Comes out the far end as brittle dried gelatin.

Milling and packing. A mill grinds the dried sheets into granules or powder, sieves sort it by particle size, and it gets packed. For animal glue specifically you'd stop caring about food-grade purity somewhere back around filtration and just aim for consistent gel strength.

# Automated Collagen-to-Glue Production Pipeline
 
## Process Specification
 
**Document type:** Process description
**Scope:** Continuous conversion of collagen-rich animal tissue into dried, granulated animal glue (technical-grade gelatin)
**Basis of design:** Continuous flow, raw material charged at intake, finished product discharged at packing
 
---
 
## 0. Process Overview
 
The pipeline converts collagen-bearing raw tissue (hide, bone, tendon, ligament, cartilage) into hydrolyzed gelatin, which is dried and milled to yield animal glue. The governing chemistry is the thermal and chemical hydrolysis of collagen — a triple-helix structural protein — into water-soluble gelatin. Keratinous material (hooves, hair) is not a feedstock, as keratin does not hydrolyze into a useful adhesive.
 
Material moves through nine unit operations grouped into three phases:
 
| Phase | Unit operations | Character |
|-------|-----------------|-----------|
| Mechanical front end | Intake & grinding, rendering & degreasing | Dry / physical separation |
| Wet processing | Chemistry soak, extraction, filtration, concentration | Aqueous chemistry |
| Finishing | Chill & extrude, drying, mill & pack | Solidification & sizing |
 
Inter-stage transport is by screw conveyor (solids) and positive-displacement or centrifugal pump (liquors). Temperature and pH are monitored and controlled at every wet stage.
 
---
 
## 1. Intake & Grinding
 
**Input:** Raw collagen-bearing tissue.
**Output:** Uniform particulate, typically 10–30 mm.
 
Raw material is charged into a receiving hopper and reduced in size by a bone crusher and hide cutter operating in series. Size reduction increases the surface-area-to-volume ratio, which directly governs the rate of downstream degreasing and collagen extraction; finer particulate extracts faster but increases fines carryover into the liquor, so particle size is a controlled trade-off rather than a "smaller is better" target.
 
**Principal equipment:** Rotary bone crusher, hide cutter, feed augers.
**Controlled parameters:** Particle size distribution, throughput rate.
 
---
 
## 2. Rendering & Degreasing
 
**Input:** Ground tissue.
**Output:** Defatted solids; separated tallow byproduct.
 
Fat is removed before extraction because lipids contaminate the gelatin liquor and depress final gel strength. Ground material passes through a steam-jacketed cooker (or continuous rendering system) that melts fat from the solid matrix. A centrifuge or continuous press then separates the liquid tallow fraction from the degreased solids. Tallow is drawn off as a saleable byproduct and leaves the glue pipeline here.
 
**Principal equipment:** Steam-jacketed / continuous cooker, decanter centrifuge or screw press.
**Controlled parameters:** Cook temperature, residence time, residual fat content of solids.
 
---
 
## 3. Chemistry Soak (Pretreatment)
 
**Input:** Defatted solids.
**Output:** Conditioned collagen matrix ready for extraction.
 
The degreased solids are conditioned in agitated tanks by either an alkaline (lime) or acid soak. This pretreatment loosens the collagen structure and hydrolyzes and removes non-collagen proteins and other soluble contaminants, raising the yield and clarity of the subsequent extraction. Alkaline conditioning is slower and typically used for tougher, more cross-linked stock; acid conditioning is faster and suits younger or less cross-linked material.
 
Reagent is metered by dosing pumps; pH probes feed a controller that maintains the target set point; transfers between soak and extraction are timed.
 
**Principal equipment:** Agitated conditioning tanks, reagent dosing pumps, pH instrumentation and controller.
**Controlled parameters:** pH, reagent concentration, soak duration, temperature.
 
---
 
## 4. Extraction
 
**Input:** Conditioned collagen matrix.
**Output:** Dilute gelatin liquor.
 
Conditioned material is transferred to temperature-controlled extraction vessels where hot water hydrolyzes collagen into gelatin, drawing it into solution. Extraction is conducted in successive stages at progressively rising temperatures; each stage recovers a further fraction of gelatin. Earlier, lower-temperature fractions yield higher-quality (higher gel strength, lighter color) gelatin; later, hotter fractions yield lower grades. Liquor and heat are moved between stages by pumps and heat exchangers.
 
**Principal equipment:** Jacketed extraction vessels (staged), heat exchangers, transfer pumps.
**Controlled parameters:** Stage temperature profile, residence time per stage, liquor concentration.
 
---
 
## 5. Filtration & Clarification
 
**Input:** Raw gelatin liquor.
**Output:** Clarified liquor.
 
Raw liquor carries fine suspended solids and residual insolubles. It is clarified by plate-and-frame filter presses and, in modern installations, ultrafiltration membranes; a centrifuge may be used ahead of filtration to remove the coarse solids load. Clarification target is set by end use: food-grade gelatin demands high clarity and low turbidity, whereas technical-grade animal glue tolerates a coarser cut, so the filtration duty is relaxed for glue production.
 
**Principal equipment:** Plate-and-frame press, ultrafiltration membrane skid, clarifying centrifuge.
**Controlled parameters:** Turbidity / clarity, filtrate flux, solids removal.
 
---
 
## 6. Concentration (Evaporation)
 
**Input:** Clarified, dilute liquor.
**Output:** Concentrated gelatin syrup.
 
The clarified liquor is dilute and must be concentrated. Water is removed in multiple-effect vacuum evaporators, which boil under reduced pressure so evaporation proceeds at low temperature. Low-temperature operation is essential: excess heat further hydrolyzes the gelatin, degrading molecular weight and reducing gel strength. The product is a thick, high-solids syrup.
 
**Principal equipment:** Multiple-effect falling-film vacuum evaporators.
**Controlled parameters:** Vacuum level, evaporation temperature, outlet solids concentration.
 
---
 
## 7. Chill & Extrude
 
**Input:** Concentrated syrup.
**Output:** Gelled noodles or ribbons.
 
The concentrated syrup is chilled below its gel point so it sets into a firm gel. The set gel is extruded into noodles or ribbons to maximize surface area for the drying stage that follows. Geometry is controlled here to give uniform, efficient drying downstream.
 
**Principal equipment:** Chiller, noodle / ribbon extruder.
**Controlled parameters:** Chill temperature, gel firmness, extrudate dimensions.
 
---
 
## 8. Drying
 
**Input:** Gelled noodles / ribbons.
**Output:** Brittle dried gelatin.
 
Extruded gel is conveyed through a drying tunnel — a belt passing through successive zones of conditioned air at controlled temperature and humidity. Air conditions are staged so moisture is removed gradually; drying too aggressively case-hardens the surface and traps moisture within, while under-drying leaves product prone to microbial spoilage. Product discharges as brittle, low-moisture dried gelatin.
 
**Principal equipment:** Multi-zone conveyor drying tunnel, air-handling / conditioning units.
**Controlled parameters:** Zone air temperature and humidity, belt speed, final moisture content.
 
---
 
## 9. Mill & Pack
 
**Input:** Dried gelatin sheets / strands.
**Output:** Sized, packed granulated product.
 
Dried material is reduced in a mill to granules or powder, classified by sieves into particle-size grades, and packed. Final product is characterized by gel strength (Bloom), viscosity, moisture, and particle size. For technical animal glue, gel strength and consistency are the governing quality attributes; food-grade specifications additionally control purity, color, and microbial load.
 
**Principal equipment:** Mill, sizing sieves / classifier, packing line.
**Controlled parameters:** Particle size grade, product moisture, gel strength, packed weight.
 
---

## Appendix B — Notes
 
- **Feedstock note:** Cattle-derived hide and bone are the predominant industrial feedstock; the process is species-agnostic with respect to collagen-bearing tissue and does not depend on any single source animal.
- **Product convergence:** This process line, with tightened clarification and purity control at stages 5–8, is materially identical to that used to produce food-grade gelatin. The principal divergence between technical animal glue and edible gelatin is the stringency of filtration and finishing, not the underlying unit operations.
- **Reversibility:** The resulting animal glue is thermally and hygroscopically reversible, which is the basis for its continued use in lutherie and antique furniture restoration.
